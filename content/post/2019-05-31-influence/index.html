It’s been a long time since I was on the Dean’s List but it happened again last month (just not the way you probably think).

Turns out I was included in a list of “Influencers.” Pretty good company, too, if you take a look at the list. What’s interesting about this is not so much that I’m an “influencer,” (although, pretty cool, I know), it’s the cascading coverage that’s stemmed from it. For example, a number of UA-published pieces and no shortage of tweets. But this got me to thinking about more than just publicity; it got me thinking about publicity.

Now, given the title of this post, I want to make it clear that I’m not suggesting being added to a list of “Influencers” makes me a scholar. That comes from years of work. And here’s my point about the publicity: I really like the notion of public scholars[], mostly because so much of what we as academics do is never seen by the world. Heck, a good chunk of it doesn’t even come out on our annual reviews. Think about all those times you’ve spent 3 hours researching so you could write one sentence. That sunken time, that invisible cost, is valuable.

This is most often now, I think, exemplified by the science communicator. While it makes sense to have an intermediary when highly technical but publicly-valauble knowledge needs dessiminating, it’s not just the hard sciences that should benefit from this sort of translation to the easily digestable. Why not education communicators? Or history communicators? Or math communicators?

Of course, we do have people like this but there still seems to be a particular and peculiar divide between science communicators and others in various fields simply writing popular (insert topic here).

Maybe it’s just me. I dunno.
